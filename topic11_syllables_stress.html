🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
Stress is the BIGGEST cause of misunderstanding in English communication. Put stress in the wrong place and listeners may not recognise the word at all! Correct stress and intonation are what make English sound natural — even more than correct individual sounds.
Syllables

What is a Syllable?

🥁 Syllable
A unit of spoken language that contains ONE vowel sound. It is the basic 'beat' of a word.
💡 'cat' has ONE beat — ONE syllable. 'tea-cher' has TWO beats — TWO syllables. Clap your hands to count syllables!
Word Number of Syllables Clap Pattern
cat 1 cat
teacher 2 tea — cher
computer 3 com — pu — ter
university 5 u — ni — ver — si — ty

🥁 Try the CLAP TEST: clap once for each syllable as you say the word. This physical method works perfectly for beginners.
Syllable Structure

Inside a Syllable: Onset, Nucleus, Coda

🏗️ Syllable Structure
Every syllable has up to 3 parts: ONSET (consonants before the vowel) + NUCLEUS (the vowel — required!) + CODA (consonants after the vowel).
💡 'cat': onset = /k/, nucleus = /æ/, coda = /t/. Every syllable MUST have a nucleus (vowel) — onset and coda are optional.
Onset (optional) Nucleus (required) Coda (optional)
/k/ /æ/ /t/
/s/ /t/ /iː/ /p/
— (no onset) /ɪ/ — (no coda)
/s/ /t/ /r/ /ɪ/ /ŋ/
💡 'strength' = /streŋθ/ — THREE consonants in the onset (str) and TWO in the coda (ŋθ). English allows complex syllable structures!
Word Stress

Word Stress: Every Word Has a Beat ♪

♪ Word Stress
In any English word with TWO or more syllables, ONE syllable is said LOUDER, LONGER, and at a HIGHER PITCH than the others. This is the STRESSED syllable.
💡 'TEAcher' — the first syllable TEA is STRESSED (louder, longer, higher). The second syllable -cher is UNSTRESSED (shorter, quieter, often becomes schwa).

Stress Patterns

Noun vs Verb Stress Pairs 🔄

🔄 Same Word — Different Stress — Different Meaning!
Some English words are spelled the same but have different stress depending on whether they are a NOUN or a VERB. This is called a STRESS MINIMAL PAIR.
PREsent (noun) ↔ preSENT (verb)
REcord (noun) ↔ reCORD (verb)
PROtest (noun) ↔ proTEST (verb)
PERmit (noun) ↔ perMIT (verb)
OBject (noun) ↔ obJECT (verb)
CONtent (noun) ↔ conTENT (adjective)
💡 In nouns, stress is usually on the FIRST syllable. In verbs, stress is often on the SECOND syllable. This is not a perfect rule but works for many common words.
Weak Forms

Unstressed Words in Connected Speech

⬇️ Weak Forms
Very common words (articles, prepositions, conjunctions, pronouns) are usually UNSTRESSED in sentences — their vowels reduce to schwa /ə/ or are shortened.
💡 Say 'I want to go' naturally — 'to' becomes /tə/, not /tuː/. Say 'the cat' — 'the' becomes /ðə/, not /ðiː/.
Word Strong Form Weak Form in Normal Speech
the /ðiː/ /ðə/ (before consonants)
a /eɪ/ /ə/
to /tuː/ /tə/
of /ɒv/ /əv/
and /ænd/ /ən/ or /n/
for /fɔː/ /fər/
at /æt/ /ət/
💡 Using strong forms for ALL words makes you sound unnatural and 'textbook'. Using weak forms is a hallmark of natural English speech.
Sentence Stress

Sentence Stress: What Do We Emphasize?

📢 Sentence Stress
In a sentence, some words are STRESSED (spoken louder/longer) and others are UNSTRESSED. CONTENT words are stressed; FUNCTION words are usually unstressed.
💡 "The CAT sat on the MAT" — stressed: CAT, sat, MAT. Unstressed: the, on, the.

📢 CONTENT Words (STRESSED)

Nouns (cat, house), Main Verbs (run, eat), Adjectives (big, happy), Adverbs (quickly, very) — these carry MEANING

⬇️ FUNCTION Words (usually unstressed)

Articles (the, a), Prepositions (in, on, at), Pronouns (he, she, it), Conjunctions (and, but, or) — these provide GRAMMAR but less meaning

💡 The stressed words carry the MOST IMPORTANT information. If you only heard the stressed words, you would still understand the sentence.
Basic Intonation

Intonation: The Music of English 🎵

🎵 Intonation
The rise and fall of the PITCH (musical note) of your voice over a sentence. Intonation tells listeners whether you are making a statement, asking a question, or showing a particular attitude.
💡 Say 'You're coming' as a STATEMENT (voice falls ↘) vs a QUESTION (voice rises ↗). Same words — different meaning!
↘
Falling Tone

Statements: 'It's raining ↘'. Commands: 'Sit down ↘'. Wh-questions: 'Where do you live ↘'

↗
Rising Tone

Yes/No questions: 'Are you coming ↗?'. Listing items (not last one): 'I have tea ↗, coffee ↗, or juice ↘'

🎵 Intonation is very important for politeness! A statement said with a RISING tone can sound like a question or uncertainty. A question said with a FALLING tone can sound rude or demanding.
