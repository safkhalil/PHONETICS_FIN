English Pronunciation in Use: Letters and Sounds
Topic 10 — English Phonetics Course
🔤 Spelling vs Pronunciation · Sound Patterns · Silent Letters · Using Dictionaries
🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
English has 26 letters but about 44 sounds. The same letter can make MANY different sounds, and the same sound can be spelled in MANY different ways. Once you understand the PATTERNS, you can predict how to pronounce most new words — and use a dictionary to confirm!
The Problem

English Spelling vs Pronunciation

Look at these examples — same letters, different sounds!
Spelling Different Words Different Sounds
'ough' tough, through, though, cough /ʌf/, /uː/, /əʊ/, /ɒf/
'ea' meat, bread, great /iː/, /e/, /eɪ/
'oo' moon, book, flood /uː/, /ʊ/, /ʌ/
'ch' church, school, chef /tʃ/, /k/, /ʃ/
'c' cat, city, ocean /k/, /s/, /ʃ/
😱 English spelling is historically irregular — words were spelled at different times and their pronunciations have changed. The IPA gives us a RELIABLE way to record pronunciation.
Pattern 1

One Letter — Many Sounds

The letter 'a' can represent FOUR different sounds:
Word Vowel Sound IPA
cat short 'a' /kæt/
cake long 'ai' diphthong /keɪk/
call long 'aw' /kɔːl/
about schwa /əˈbaʊt/
The letter 'c' can be:
Word Sound Rule
cat, car, cup /k/ 'c' before a, o, u
city, circle, cent /s/ 'c' before e, i, y
ocean, special /ʃ/ 'c' + 'i' in some words
💡 This is why we use IPA! The letter tells us only the SPELLING. The IPA tells us the actual SOUND.
Pattern 2

One Sound — Many Spellings

🎯 The IPA Solution
The IPA symbol /iː/ always represents the same sound, no matter how it is spelled. Here are ALL the ways English spells the /iː/ sound:
Spelling Examples
ee see, feet, tree, sleep
ea sea, meat, read, each
e + consonant + e these, here, compete
ie believe, achieve, field
ei receive, ceiling
ey key, monkey, valley
i in unstressed syllables machine, police, magazine
e at end (rare) be, me, we, she, he
💡 ONE sound /iː/ — NINE different spellings! This is why relying on spelling alone for pronunciation is unreliable.
Silent Letters

Beware — Silent Letters! 🤫

🤫 Silent Letters
Letters that are written but NOT pronounced. English has many silent letters because of historical spelling changes — the spelling was 'frozen' when people stopped saying the sound.
💡 'knife' — the 'k' was pronounced in Old English! Over time, the /k/ was dropped from speech, but the spelling stayed.
Silent Letter Examples IPA
silent k knife, know, knee, knit /naɪf/, /nəʊ/, /niː/
silent w write, wrap, wrong, wrist /raɪt/, /ræp/, /rɒŋ/
silent b lamb, thumb, comb, bomb /læm/, /θʌm/
silent g sign, align, foreign /saɪn/, /əˈlaɪn/
silent l walk, talk, calm, half /wɔːk/, /tɔːk/
silent h hour, honest, heir /aʊər/, /ɒnɪst/
💡 The safest solution: ALWAYS check the IPA in a dictionary before assuming how a word is pronounced.
Pattern 3

Common Consonant Spelling Patterns

Spelling Sound Examples
sh /ʃ/ ship, wash, fashion
ch /tʃ/ or /k/ church BUT school, chrome
ph /f/ phone, photo, phrase, dolphin
gh /f/ or silent enough /ɪˈnʌf/, light /laɪt/
ck /k/ back, duck, lock, neck
ng /ŋ/ or /ŋɡ/ sing /sɪŋ/, finger /fɪŋɡər/
wh /w/ or /h/ where, what (US: /hw/)
th /θ/ or /ð/ think (voiceless), this (voiced)
💡 'ph' = /f/ always in English: phone, photo, phrase, trophy, dolphin. Why? Because these words came from Greek, where φ (phi) = /f/.
Using Dictionaries

Reading IPA in a Real Dictionary 📖

📖 How to Use Your Dictionary for Pronunciation
Every modern English dictionary shows the IPA pronunciation of each word. Learning to read it is one of the most important skills you can develop in this course!
Example dictionary entry: teach /tiːtʃ/ verb
teacher /ˈtiːtʃər/ — The ˈ mark = PRIMARY STRESS on the next syllable. Always stressed on TEA- not -cher.
look up /lʊk ʌp/ phrasal verb
IPA Symbol What it Tells You
ˈ before a syllable Primary stress: SAY this syllable LOUDER and LONGER
ˌ before a syllable Secondary stress (less common)
(r) in brackets The 'r' is pronounced in American English but silent in British
/ / around everything This is the phonemic transcription
Pattern 4

Vowel Sound-Spelling: Key Patterns

Vowel Pattern Sound Examples
a + consonant + e /eɪ/ diphthong cake, make, name, same
i + consonant + e /aɪ/ diphthong bike, time, fine, like
o + consonant + e /əʊ/ diphthong home, note, bone, stone
a + ll /ɔː/ ball, call, tall, fall
a + r /ɑː/ car, far, star, park
i + r, u + r, e + r /ɜː/ bird, nurse, her, first
💡 The 'magic e' rule: adding 'e' at the end of a word often LENGTHENS the vowel: 'cap' → 'cape', 'kit' → 'kite', 'hop' → 'hope'.
