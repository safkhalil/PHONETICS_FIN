🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
Real spoken English sounds VERY different from how words look on paper — or even from how you say them individually. Native speakers LINK words together, DROP sounds, and CHANGE sounds in fast speech. This lesson helps you understand and produce natural, connected English.
Connected Speech

What is Connected Speech?

🔗 Connected Speech
When we speak naturally and quickly, words do not stay separate — they CONNECT and influence each other. Sounds change, combine, or disappear depending on neighboring sounds.
💡 Slowly: "I want to go" — Naturally: "I wanna go." The words flow together and sounds change.
🔗

Linking

Sounds connect across word boundaries

🔄

Assimilation

A sound CHANGES to match its neighbour

✂️

Elision

A sound is DROPPED

➕

Intrusion

An EXTRA sound appears between two vowels

💡 These are not MISTAKES — they are NORMAL features of natural English speech. All native speakers use them automatically.
Linking

Linking — Words Flow Together 🔗

🔗 Consonant-to-Vowel Linking
When a word ends in a CONSONANT sound and the next word starts with a VOWEL sound, the consonant LINKS to the vowel — it sounds like the consonant belongs to the second word.
💡 'an apple' = sounds like 'a napple'. 'turn off' = sounds like 'tur noff'.
Written Linked Pronunciation Rule
an apple /ə ˈnæpl/ n + vowel
comes out /kʌm ˈzaʊt/ z + vowel
hold on /həʊl ˈdɒn/ d + vowel
in a few /ɪn ə ˈfjuː/ n + schwa + f
pick it up /pɪ kɪt ˈʌp/ k+vowel, t+vowel
🔗 Linking is WHY English sometimes sounds like 'one long word' — the words are literally joined together in natural speech!
Weak Forms in Speech

Weak Forms: Words Get Shorter in Sentences

⬇️ Function Words Go 'Weak'
Common grammar words (the, a, to, of, and, at) are usually UNSTRESSED and REDUCED in natural conversation. If you say these words in their full form every time, you will sound unnatural — like reading from a textbook.
Written Form Strong Form Weak Form in Natural Speech
I want to go /tuː/ 'wanna' /wɒnə/ or /tə/
going to /tuː/ 'gonna' /ɡɒnə/
have to — 'hafta' /hæftə/
want to — 'wanna' /wɒnə/
and /ænd/ /ən/ or /n/
of /ɒv/ /əv/ or just /ə/
💡 'Gonna', 'wanna', 'hafta' are NOT bad grammar — they are natural spoken forms. In FORMAL WRITING, use 'going to', 'want to', 'have to'. In CONVERSATION, the weak forms are normal and expected.
Assimilation

Assimilation — Sounds Influence Each Other 🔄

🔄 Assimilation
A sound CHANGES to become more like a NEIGHBORING sound. This happens because the mouth prepares for the next sound BEFORE finishing the current one.
💡 'ten boys' → sounds like 'TEM boys'. The /n/ changes to /m/ because /b/ (bilabial) pulls the preceding nasal to a bilabial /m/.
Written Assimilated Form Why
ten boys 'tem boys' /n/ → /m/ before bilabial /b/
ten girls 'teng girls' /n/ → /ŋ/ before velar /g/
handbag 'hambag' /n/ → /m/ before bilabial /b/
this week 'thiss week' (extended /s/) voicing assimilation
good boy 'goob boy' /d/ → /b/ before bilabial
🔄 Assimilation often happens at WORD BOUNDARIES — the end of one word changes to match the start of the next. This is an automatic, unconscious process in fast speech.
Elision

Elision — Sounds Get Dropped ✂️

✂️ Elision
A sound is OMITTED (dropped) in fast speech. This usually happens to consonants in awkward clusters or in very common, unstressed words.
💡 'next day' → 'nex day' (/t/ dropped because /k/, /s/, /t/ in a row is hard to say quickly). 'last night' → 'lass night'.
Written Elided Form Sound Dropped
next door 'nex door' /t/ dropped
last time 'lass time' /t/ dropped
most students 'moss students' /t/ dropped
sandwich 'sanwich' /d/ dropped
handbag 'hanbag' /d/ dropped
temperature 'temprature' first /ə/ dropped
comfortable 'comftable' /ər/ dropped
✂️ Elision is common in FAST SPEECH. In CAREFUL, SLOW speech, all sounds may be pronounced. Being aware of elision helps you understand fast native speech much better.
Intrusion

Intrusion — Extra Sounds Appear ➕

➕ Intrusion
An EXTRA sound is ADDED between two vowel sounds to make the transition smoother. This is an unconscious process. Three sounds can intrude: /r/, /j/, and /w/.
💡 'saw it' → 'saw(r) it' — an /r/ appears even though 'saw' doesn't end in the letter 'r'. This is called 'intrusive r'.
Written Intrusive Sound Examples
vowel ending in /ɑː ɔː ə/ + vowel /r/ intrudes saw_it → 'saw(r)it', the idea_of → 'the idea(r)of'
vowel ending in /iː ɪ eɪ/ + vowel /j/ intrudes he_asked → 'he(j)asked', I_am → 'I(j)am'
vowel ending in /uː ʊ/ + vowel /w/ intrudes do_it → 'do(w)it', who_is → 'who(w)is'
💡 INTRUSIVE /r/ is very noticeable in non-rhotic accents (like standard British English). The speaker adds /r/ NOT because it's in the spelling, but because it makes the transition between vowels smoother.
Listening Strategies

Understanding Fast Native Speech 👂

👂 How to Understand Fast English Speech
When listening to native English speakers in films, podcasts, or real conversations, use these strategies:
1. 🎯 Expect LINKING — words connect. 'Turn it off' sounds like 'tur ni toff'.
2. ✂️ Expect ELISION — sounds disappear. 'Last night' sounds like 'lass night'.
3. ⬇️ Expect WEAK FORMS — function words are short. 'Have you eaten?' sounds like 'Havya eaten?'.
4. 🔄 Use CONTEXT — even if you miss a sound, the meaning is usually clear from context.
💡 Authentic listening practice is the best way to improve: films, podcasts, BBC News, conversations. Start slowly — use subtitles, then wean yourself off them.
Speaking Naturally

Producing Natural Connected Speech 🗣️

🗣️ Tips for More Natural English Pronunciation
You do not need to copy every feature of fast speech immediately. Start with these basics:

Link words together

End consonant + start vowel = connect them. Practice: 'turn off' 'an apple' 'hold on'

Use weak forms

Reduce 'to', 'the', 'and', 'of', 'a' to their weak forms. 'I wanna go'. 'The /ðə/ cat'.

Stress content words

Emphasize the important words in a sentence. Reduce the grammar words.

Chunk sentences

Group words into PHRASES, not individual words. 'I'll see you / later today'

💡 The goal is INTELLIGIBLE, NATURAL English — not perfect imitation of a specific accent. Your personality and identity come through your voice!
