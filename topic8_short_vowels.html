Motivation

Why does this lesson matter?

🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
English has only 5 vowel LETTERS (a, e, i, o, u) but about 20 vowel SOUNDS! Most pronunciation problems come from vowels, not consonants. Learning the vowel chart and short vowels will help you distinguish words like 'sit' vs 'seat', 'bed' vs 'bad', and 'cup' vs 'cap'.
What is a Vowel?

Vowels vs Consonants

🎵 Vowel
A sound where the air flows FREELY through the mouth with no blocking or squeezing. The shape of the mouth (tongue height and position, lip rounding) determines which vowel you produce.
💡 Say 'ahhh' — feel how freely the air flows. Now say 'sss' — feel the restriction. 'ahhh' is a vowel; 'sss' is a consonant!

VOWELS

Air flows freely. No blocking. Described by: tongue height (high/mid/low), tongue position (front/back), lip shape (rounded/unrounded)

CONSONANTS

Air is blocked or restricted. Described by: voicing, place of articulation, manner of articulation

💡 In every syllable, the VOWEL is the core — the 'nucleus'. You CANNOT have a syllable without a vowel (or vowel-like sound).
The Vowel Chart

The International Vowel Chart (Simplified)

The Vowel Chart is a map of the MOUTH. It shows where the tongue is positioned when you make each vowel:

Tongue Position on the Vowel Chart:

FRONT (tongue forward) CENTER BACK (tongue back)
HIGH: /iː ɪ/ — HIGH: /uː ʊ/
MID: /e/ /ə/ /ɜː/ MID: /ɔː/
LOW: /æ/ /ʌ/ LOW: /ɑː ɒ/
🗺️ FRONT = tongue pushed toward the front of the mouth. BACK = tongue pulled to the back. HIGH = tongue raised up. LOW = tongue lowered.
Cardinal Vowels

Primary Cardinal Vowels — Reference Points

🎯 What are Cardinal Vowels?
Cardinal vowels are REFERENCE POINTS — like compass directions for vowels. Linguists use them to describe and compare vowels in different languages. There are 8 primary cardinal vowels.
Number & Symbol Tongue Position Example in a Language
CV1 /i/ High front unrounded 'see' in English
CV2 /e/ High-mid front similar to French 'été'
CV3 /ɛ/ Low-mid front 'bed' in English
CV4 /a/ Low front 'car' in some accents
CV5 /ɑ/ Low back 'father' in English
CV6 /ɔ/ Low-mid back rounded 'more' in English
CV7 /o/ High-mid back rounded similar to Spanish 'no'
CV8 /u/ High back rounded 'moon' in English
💡 English vowels are compared to these 8 reference points. They help phoneticians describe where English vowels sit on the vowel chart.

💡 The SCHWA /ə/ is the MOST COMMON vowel sound in English. It appears in UNSTRESSED syllables only — 'about', 'the', 'banana', 'professor'.
Short Vowels 1-3

Close-up: /ɪ/, /e/, and /æ/

/ɪ/ — Short High Front Vowel
Tongue HIGH and FRONT, lips spread slightly. SHORTER and more relaxed than /iː/.
💡 sit /sɪt/ · big /bɪɡ/ · fish /fɪʃ/ · him /hɪm/ · this /ðɪs/
/e/ — Short Mid Front Vowel
Tongue MID and FRONT, lips slightly spread. As in 'bed'.
💡 bed /bed/ · pen /pen/ · red /red/ · ten /ten/ · dress /dres/
/æ/ — Short Low Front Vowel
Tongue LOW and FRONT, mouth relatively open. Often called the 'flat a'.
💡 cat /kæt/ · man /mæn/ · bag /bæɡ/ · hand /hænd/ · black /blæk/
Short Vowels 4-6

Close-up: /ʌ/, /ɒ/, and /ʊ/

/ʌ/ — Short Mid Central Vowel
Tongue MID and in the CENTER of the mouth. Lips neutral.
💡 cup /kʌp/ · sun /sʌn/ · love /lʌv/ · but /bʌt/ · money /mʌni/
/ɒ/ — Short Low Back Vowel
Tongue LOW and BACK, lips slightly rounded. As in 'top'.
💡 top /tɒp/ · dog /dɒɡ/ · hot /hɒt/ · on /ɒn/ · not /nɒt/
/ʊ/ — Short High Back Vowel
Tongue HIGH and BACK, lips lightly rounded. Shorter and more relaxed than /uː/.
💡 book /bʊk/ · put /pʊt/ · good /ɡʊd/ · look /lʊk/ · foot /fʊt/
The Schwa

The Schwa /ə/ — The Star of English Vowels ⭐

⭐ /ə/ — The Schwa
The most common vowel in English! The tongue is in the CENTRE of the mouth — neutral position. Lips are relaxed. It ONLY appears in UNSTRESSED syllables.
💡 'about' = /əˈbaʊt/ · 'the' = /ðə/ · 'banana' = /bəˈnɑːnə/ · 'teacher' = /ˈtiːtʃə/
Word IPA Transcription — notice the schwa!
about /əˈbaʊt/ — first syllable
banana /bəˈnɑːnə/ — first AND last syllables
teacher /ˈtiːtʃə/ — last syllable
of /əv/ — the whole word!
the /ðə/ — the whole word (unstressed form)
⭐ In natural spoken English, about 10–15% of ALL vowel sounds are schwa! Mastering the schwa will make your English sound much more natural.

Minimal Pair Vowel Practice 🎯

👂 Listen and Compare
These minimal pairs differ by ONLY the vowel sound. Can you hear (and say!) the difference?
sit /ɪ/ ↔ seat /iː/
bed /e/ ↔ bad /æ/
cat /æ/ ↔ cut /ʌ/
top /ɒ/ ↔ tape /eɪ/
book /ʊ/ ↔ boot /uː/
man /æ/ ↔ men /e/
💡 Try this: put your hand under your chin. For LOW vowels (/æ/ /ɒ/ /ʌ/), your jaw drops more. For HIGH vowels (/ɪ/ /ʊ/), your jaw stays up. This physical feedback helps you feel the difference!
