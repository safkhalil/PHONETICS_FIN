Motivation
🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
You already know WHERE sounds are made (place). Now learn HOW sounds are made (manner). Together, place + manner + voicing = the complete phonetic description of any consonant. Example: /p/ = voiceless + bilabial + STOP.
Key Concept

What is Manner of Articulation?

💨 Manner of Articulation
HOW the airflow is modified or shaped to produce a sound. Different manners create very different types of sounds.
💡 Compare /p/ (air completely blocked then released) and /s/ (air squeezed through a narrow gap = hissing). Same general area, different manner!

There are 5 main manners of articulation for English consonants: Stops, Fricatives, Affricates, Approximants, and Nasals.

💡 Topic 5 covers Stops, Fricatives, and Approximants. Affricates and Nasals are covered in Topic 7.
Manner 1

Stops (Plosives) 💥

💥 Stop (Plosive)
The airflow is COMPLETELY BLOCKED, then SUDDENLY RELEASED. Like a small explosion!
💡 Say /p/ — your lips block ALL the air, then release it. Say /t/ — your tongue tip blocks the air at the alveolar ridge.

English Stop Consonants

Voiceless Voiced Place
/p/ pan /b/ ban Bilabial
/t/ top /d/ dog Alveolar
/k/ cat /g/ go Velar
🔑 3 voiceless stops + 3 voiced stops = 6 stops total in English.
Manner 2

Fricatives 🌬️

🌬️ Fricative
The articulators come VERY CLOSE together but do NOT touch completely. Air is SQUEEZED through a narrow gap — creating a hissing or buzzing sound.
💡 Say "sssss" — feel the air squeezing between your tongue and the alveolar ridge. That friction = FRICATIVE!
Voiceless Voiced Place
/f/ fan /v/ van Labiodental
/θ/ think /ð/ this Dental
/s/ sun /z/ zoo Alveolar
/ʃ/ ship /ʒ/ measure Post-alveolar
/h/ hat — Glottal
💡 /h/ has no voiced pair in English. It is the only glottal fricative.
Manner 3

Approximants 🌊

🌊 Approximant
The articulators come CLOSE together but the airflow is NOT restricted enough to create friction. The sound is smooth and vowel-like.
💡 Compare /w/ in 'wet' — it sounds almost like a vowel but works as a consonant at the start of syllables.
Symbol Example word + description
/j/ 'yes', 'you' — tongue body near hard palate
/w/ 'wet', 'away' — lips rounded, back of tongue raised
/r/ 'red', 'car' — tongue curves back slightly
/l/ 'love', 'tall' — tongue tip at alveolar ridge, air flows around the SIDES
💡 /l/ is special — it is a LATERAL approximant because air flows over the SIDES of the tongue, not through the middle.
Voicing Pairs

Voiced and Voiceless: The Complete Picture

🎯 The Voicing Principle
For most manners of articulation, sounds come in PAIRS: one voiceless and one voiced. The place and manner are the same — only voicing is different. This is why minimal pairs exist!
Manner Voiceless Voiced Example Pair
Stop /p/, /t/, /k/ /b/, /d/, /g/ pin / bin
Fricative /f/, /θ/, /s/, /ʃ/ /v/, /ð/, /z/, /ʒ/ fine / vine
Approximant (mostly voiced) /j/, /w/, /r/, /l/ —
💡 Nasals (/m/, /n/, /ŋ/) are always VOICED — they have no voiceless pair in English.
Describing Consonants

The 3-Part Description System

Every English consonant can be described using 3 things: VOICING + PLACE + MANNER

/p/

voiceless + bilabial + stop

/z/

voiced + alveolar + fricative

/l/

voiced + alveolar + lateral approximant

🎯 This is the standard way linguists describe consonants. Learn this formula and you can describe ANY consonant in ANY language!
