🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
English spelling is one of the most irregular in the world. The same letters can sound completely different! The IPA solves this problem forever: ONE symbol ALWAYS equals ONE sound. Once you know the IPA, you can read any English word correctly — even words you have never seen before.
The Problem

Why Can't We Use Regular Spelling?

Look at these words — all spelled with 'ough' but all DIFFERENT sounds:

Word Pronunciation
tough /tʌf/ — rhymes with 'stuff'
through /θruː/ — rhymes with 'blue'
though /ðəʊ/ — rhymes with 'so'
cough /kɒf/ — rhymes with 'off'
thought /θɔːt/ — rhymes with 'sort'
😱 Five spellings. Five different sounds. This is why phoneticians created the IPA!
The Solution

What is the IPA?

🗺️ The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)
A system of symbols where EVERY symbol represents EXACTLY ONE sound. Created in 1888 and updated regularly. Used by linguists, language teachers, and dictionaries worldwide.
💡 In the IPA, the word 'fish' is written /fɪʃ/ — always, in any dictionary, in any country!
🎯

One Symbol = One Sound

No confusion. /p/ is always /p/.

🌍

Works for ALL Languages

The same symbols describe sounds in English, Arabic, Chinese, and more.

📚

Used in All Dictionaries

Cambridge, Oxford, Merriam-Webster — all use the IPA.

Notation

Square Brackets [ ] vs Slashes / /

[p]
Phonetic transcription — narrow. Shows exact sounds including small details like aspiration: [pʰɪn]
/p/
Phonemic transcription — broad. Shows only meaning-changing sounds: /pɪn/
📌 For most purposes in this course, we use PHONEMIC transcription with slashes / /. Square brackets [ ] are used when we need more detail.
IPA Consonants 1

English Consonants — Group 1

English has 24 consonant phonemes. Here are the first group:

Symbol Word Example Spelling
/p/ pen, cap p
/b/ bat, cab b
/t/ top, cat t
/d/ dog, bad d
/k/ cat, back c, k, ck
/g/ go, bag g
/f/ fan, off f, ph
/v/ van, live v
💡 Notice /k/ can be spelled c, k, or ck — but the IPA symbol is always the same: /k/.
IPA Consonants 2

English Consonants — Group 2

Symbol Example Note
/s/ sit, bus voiceless
/z/ zoo, buzz voiced pair of /s/
/ʃ/ ship, wash sh sound — voiceless
/ʒ/ measure, vision voiced pair of /ʃ/
/h/ hat, ahead voiceless, glottal
/tʃ/ church, watch affricate — voiceless
/dʒ/ judge, age affricate — voiced
/θ/ think, bath th — voiceless
/ð/ this, breathe th — voiced
💡 /θ/ and /ð/ are BOTH spelled 'th' in English — but they are TWO different sounds! 'think' /θɪŋk/ vs 'this' /ðɪs/.
IPA Consonants 3

English Consonants — Group 3 (Sonorants)

Symbol Example Type
/m/ man, swim nasal
/n/ no, sun nasal
/ŋ/ sing, long nasal — back of mouth
/l/ love, tall lateral
/r/ red, car approximant
/j/ yes, you approximant
/w/ wet, away approximant
💡 /ŋ/ never starts a word in English — it only appears in the middle or at the END: 'sing' /sɪŋ/, 'finger' /fɪŋɡə/.
