Why does this lesson matter?

🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
Affricates are in everyday English words like 'church', 'cheese', 'judge', and 'age'. Nasal stops are in 'man', 'no', and 'sing'. Getting these right is essential for clear English pronunciation — and understanding why 'pin' sounds different from 'spin' will help you sound more natural!
Manner 4

What are Affricates? 🎺

🎺 Affricate
An affricate starts as a STOP (air completely blocked) and immediately becomes a FRICATIVE (air squeezed through a narrow gap). It is one sound that combines two movements.
💡 /tʃ/ in 'church' = stop /t/ + fricative /ʃ/ happening together. /dʒ/ in 'judge' = stop /d/ + fricative /ʒ/.
🔇

/tʃ/ — Voiceless Affricate

church, cheese, watch, choose, teacher

Same place as /ʃ/ — post-alveolar
🔊

/dʒ/ — Voiced Affricate

judge, jam, age, bridge, soldier

Voiced pair of /tʃ/
💡 English has only TWO affricate phonemes: /tʃ/ and /dʒ/. They are both post-alveolar.
Affricates in Words

Recognizing /tʃ/ and /dʒ/ in Spelling

Affricates can be spelled in MANY different ways — but the sound is always the same:
Sound Common Spelling Examples
/tʃ/ ch church, cheese, chair, child
/tʃ/ tch watch, catch, match, kitchen
/tʃ/ t + ure picture, nature, future, creature
/dʒ/ j jam, jump, just, jacket
/dʒ/ g + e/i/y age, giant, gym, bridge
/dʒ/ dge judge, bridge, fridge, hedge
💡 This is another reason why IPA is so useful! Despite all these different spellings, the sounds /tʃ/ and /dʒ/ each have just ONE IPA symbol.
Aspiration

What is Aspiration? 💨

💨 Aspiration [h]
A small puff of air that comes AFTER a voiceless stop consonant when it is at the START of a stressed syllable. In IPA, we mark it with a small raised [h]: [pʰ].
💡 Hold a thin piece of paper in front of your mouth and say "pin". The paper moves! Now say "spin" — it barely moves. This puff is aspiration!

ASPIRATED

[pʰ]

In 'pin', 'top', 'key' — at the start of a stressed syllable — strong puff of air

UNASPIRATED

[p]

In 'spin', 'stop', 'sky' — after /s/ — no puff of air

💡 Aspiration does NOT change meaning in English — [pʰ] and [p] are ALLOPHONES of /p/.
Aspiration Rules

When Are Stops Aspirated in English?

The rule for aspiration in English:
Context Result
Start of a STRESSED syllable (no /s/ before it) ASPIRATED [pʰ], [tʰ], [kʰ]
After /s/ in the same syllable UNASPIRATED [p], [t], [k]
pin [pʰɪn] ↔ spin [spɪn]
top [tʰɒp] ↔ stop [stɒp]
key [kʰiː] ↔ sky [skaɪ]
🌍 Aspiration in Other Languages
In many languages (like Arabic, French, Spanish), stops are NEVER aspirated. If you speak these languages, you may not aspirate English stops — this can make your pronunciation sound slightly different from native English. Practice adding that puff of air!

Nasal Stops: Sounds Through the Nose 👃

👃 Nasal Stop
The mouth is CLOSED (like a stop — air is blocked by the lips or tongue). But the velum (soft palate) LOWERS, so air flows through the NOSE. All nasal stops are VOICED.
💡 Say 'mmm' with your mouth closed. Air comes out through your nose. Now pinch your nose — you cannot continue the sound! That's a nasal stop.
👄

/m/ Bilabial Nasal

man, swim, home, time

LIPS closed — air through nose
👅

/n/ Alveolar Nasal

no, sun, under, ten

TONGUE TIP at alveolar ridge — air through nose
🔙

/ŋ/ Velar Nasal

sing, ring, long, finger

BACK OF TONGUE at velum — air through nose
The /ŋ/ Sound

The Tricky /ŋ/ — The Velar Nasal

🔙 /ŋ/ — Voiced Velar Nasal
The back of the tongue touches the velum (soft palate) and closes off the mouth. Air flows through the nose. This sound is spelled 'ng' or 'n' before /k/ or /g/.
💡 'sing' /sɪŋ/ — 'ring' /rɪŋ/ — 'finger' /fɪŋɡər/ — 'bank' /bæŋk/
Fact Detail
Appears at end of syllable sing, ring, long, hang, strong
Appears in the middle finger, singer, longer, anger
NEVER at the start No English words begin with /ŋ/
/n/ before /k/ or /g/ bank = /bæŋk/, uncle = /ʌŋkl/
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE: Many learners add an extra /g/ sound: 'sɪŋɡ' instead of 'sɪŋ'. In standard English 'sing', the /g/ is SILENT — only /ŋ/ at the end!
Summary

Topic 7: Complete Consonant Summary

/tʃ/ Voiceless Affricate

church, cheese, watch — voiceless post-alveolar

/dʒ/ Voiced Affricate

judge, jam, age — voiced post-alveolar

Aspiration [pʰ]

Extra puff of air after voiceless stops at the start of stressed syllables — allophone, not a new phoneme

/m/ Bilabial Nasal

Lips closed, air through nose — 'man, swim'

/n/ /ŋ/ Nasals

Alveolar /n/ anywhere. Velar /ŋ/ only in the MIDDLE or END of syllables — NEVER the start
