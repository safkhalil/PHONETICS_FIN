Central vs Lateral

Central vs Lateral Airflow

🌊 Central vs Lateral
In most consonants, air flows through the CENTRE of the mouth (central). In /l/, air flows over the SIDES (lateral) of the tongue while the tip touches the alveolar ridge.
💡 Say 'la la la' — feel how your tongue tip stays on the ridge but air escapes around the SIDES.

🌊 LATERAL — /l/

Tongue TIP touches alveolar ridge. Air flows around the SIDES. Only ONE lateral in standard English: /l/. Examples: love, tall, ball, milk

➡️ CENTRAL — all other sounds

Air flows through the CENTRE of the mouth. All other English consonants and vowels are central. Examples: /t/ /d/ /n/ /r/ /s/ /z/ and all vowels

💡 Say 'nil' vs 'nit' — in 'nil', the final /l/ lets air escape around your tongue sides (lateral). In 'nit', the /t/ stops all airflow.
Clear L vs Dark L

The Two Allophones of /l/

🔦 Clear /l/ vs Dark /ɫ/
English /l/ has TWO allophones depending on position in the word.
💡 'light' starts with CLEAR l (before a vowel). 'full' ends with DARK l (after a vowel or before a consonant).
Allophone Position Examples
Clear [l] Before a vowel or /j/ light, love, please, million
Dark [ɫ] After a vowel or before a consonant full, ball, milk, help, old
💡 In many English accents (like Southern British English), the dark /l/ can even become a vowel-like sound! This is called L-VOCALIZATION. 'milk' sounds like 'miwk'.
The Rhotic /r/

The English /r/ — A Special Approximant

🔄 Rhotic /r/
The English /r/ is a voiced APPROXIMANT — the tongue curves slightly backward or bunches up, but does NOT touch the roof of the mouth. Air flows freely.
💡 English /r/ is very different from the rolled 'r' in Spanish ('perro') or the tapped 'r' in Arabic!
Type Description Example Language
English /r/ Tongue curves back — approximant — NO contact English
Tapped /r/ [ɾ] Tongue briefly taps the alveolar ridge ONCE Spanish 'pero', American English 'butter'
Trill /r/ [r] Tongue vibrates rapidly against the alveolar ridge Spanish 'perro', Arabic, Italian
💡 For English /r/, remember: the tongue NEVER touches the palate — it just curves back slightly.
Taps and Trills

Taps, Flaps and Trills

👉

Tap / Flap [ɾ]

The tongue TIP makes ONE VERY QUICK touch against the alveolar ridge. It's like a very fast /t/ or /d/.

Example: Spanish 'pero' (but), American English 'butter' [bʌɾɚ]
🎵

Trill [r]

The tongue tip VIBRATES rapidly against the alveolar ridge. Multiple contacts in rapid succession.

Example: Spanish 'perro' (dog), Arabic ر, Italian 'terra'
💡 IMPORTANT: Standard English does NOT use taps or trills — English /r/ is an approximant. But knowing these helps you understand other languages and accents!
Secondary Articulation

Secondary Articulation

🎭 Secondary Articulation
An EXTRA modification added to a sound, in addition to its main (primary) place and manner of articulation.
💡 The /w/ in 'wet' has TWO places: main = velar (back of tongue raised) AND secondary = labial (lips rounded). This is called LABIALIZATION.
Type Description English Example
Labialization Lips are ROUNDED in addition to the main articulation /w/ in 'wet'
Palatalization Tongue body moves toward HARD PALATE in addition /j/ influence in some words
Nasalization Velum lowers — air goes through NOSE vowels before nasal consonants (e.g., 'man')
💡 Secondary articulations are subtle. You do not usually HEAR them as separate sounds — they are extra features of the main sound.
Labialization & Nasalization

Two Key Secondary Articulations

💧 Labialization
The lips become ROUNDED while the main articulation is made elsewhere. /w/ is labialized because the back of the tongue rises (primary = velar) AND the lips round (secondary = labial).
💡 Say 'wet' slowly — notice how your lips round BEFORE your tongue does anything. This simultaneous rounding is labialization.
👃 Nasalization
The soft palate (velum) LOWERS slightly, allowing some air to flow through the nose even during a non-nasal sound. This often happens to vowels that come BEFORE nasal consonants.
💡 In 'man', the vowel /æ/ is slightly nasalized because the velum starts lowering for the final /n/.
