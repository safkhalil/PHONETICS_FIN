🌟 Why does this lesson matter?
Before you can study English sounds, you need to know WHERE those sounds come from in your body! Every English sound is made by a specific combination of movements inside your mouth and throat. This lesson is your roadmap.
Overview

Your Body is a Sound Machine 🎶

When you talk, your whole body works together. Air starts in your LUNGS, passes your VOICE BOX, and is shaped by your MOUTH and NOSE into the sounds of language.

🫁

Lungs

They push air upward to make all speech sounds.

🔊

Larynx (Voice Box)

Contains the vocal cords — they vibrate to make your voice.

👄

Vocal Tract

Your mouth, nose, and throat — they shape the sound into specific sounds.

Step 1

The Airstream Mechanism

💨 Airstream Mechanism
The source of air that drives all speech sounds. In English (and most languages), we use air pushed OUT from the lungs.
💡 This is called the PULMONIC EGRESSIVE airstream: pulmonic = from the lungs; egressive = going out.
💡 Every time you speak, you are breathing OUT. Try saying 'Hello' while breathing IN — it's almost impossible!

There are other types of airstream (clicks, implosives) used in other languages, but they are rare in English.

Step 2

The Larynx (Voice Box)

🔊 The Larynx
A structure in your throat that contains the VOCAL CORDS (also called vocal folds). The larynx is the part that 'sticks out' in men — often called the Adam's apple.
💡 Put your hand gently on your throat and swallow. You will feel your larynx move up and down!

The vocal cords are two small muscles inside the larynx. They can either: OPEN (air passes freely — voiceless sound) or CLOSE and VIBRATE (voice is added — voiced sound)

Step 3

Voiced vs Voiceless Sounds

V

VOICED

The vocal cords VIBRATE. You can feel vibration if you touch your throat.

/b/ /d/ /g/ /v/ /z/
∅

VOICELESS

The vocal cords are OPEN. Air passes freely — no vibration.

/p/ /t/ /k/ /f/ /s/
🖐️ Try it! Put your finger on your throat. Say 'ssss' — no vibration (voiceless /s/). Now say 'zzzz' — feel the buzz? That's voiced /z/!
Step 4

The Vocal Tract

🗣️ The Vocal Tract
The space from your larynx up to your lips and nostrils. This is where sounds are SHAPED into specific consonants and vowels.
💡 The same vowels and consonants can be very different just from changing tongue position!

🏃 Active Articulators (they MOVE)

  • Tongue (tip, blade, back, root)
  • Lower jaw
  • Soft palate (velum)
  • Lips

🧱 Passive Articulators (fixed targets)

  • Upper teeth
  • Alveolar ridge (bumpy ridge behind upper front teeth)
  • Hard palate
  • Velum (in its fixed position)
Step 5

The Tongue: Your Most Important Articulator

The tongue is divided into 4 parts, each important for different sounds:

Part Role & Sounds
Tip (front) Makes /t/ /d/ /n/ /l/ /s/ /z/ at the alveolar ridge
Blade Makes /ʃ/ /ʒ/ and post-alveolar sounds
Body (front) Makes palatal sounds like /j/
Back Makes velar sounds like /k/ /g/ /ŋ/
💡 The tongue moves dozens of times per second when you speak naturally. Most of this movement is unconscious!
Summary

The Journey of a Speech Sound

Follow the path of a sound:
1️⃣ LUNGS push air out (pulmonic egressive) → 2️⃣ Air passes through the LARYNX → 3️⃣ VOCAL CORDS vibrate (voiced) or stay open (voiceless) → 4️⃣ Air enters the VOCAL TRACT → 5️⃣ TONGUE, LIPS, and other articulators shape the air → 6️⃣ Sound exits through MOUTH or NOSE

Pulmonic Airstream
Air from the lungs — drives all English speech
Vocal Cords
Vibrating = voiced; Open = voiceless
Active Articulators
Tongue, lips, jaw — they move to shape sounds
Passive Articulators
Teeth, alveolar ridge, palate — fixed targets
Practice

Feel Your Own Speech Organs 🤲

🌟 Physical Awareness Activity
Try these activities right now to connect the theory to your own body!
1. 👋 Put your hand in front of your mouth — say 'puh'. Feel a puff of air! (pulmonic egressive)
2. 🤚 Touch your throat and say 'zzzzz' then 'ssssss' — feel the difference (voiced vs voiceless)
3. 👅 Touch your upper front teeth with your tongue tip, then say 'th' as in 'the'
4. 👄 Say 'mmm' and close your lips completely — your vocal cords are vibrating and air exits through your nose!
💡 These physical connections will help you remember the theory much better than reading alone.
